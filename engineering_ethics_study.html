Ch. 1 Engineering Ethics: Introduction ▼
Tesla Case Study

Key Case Discussion

The Tesla autopilot case raises core ethics questions: who bears moral responsibility when autonomous systems fail? Key issues include realistic driver override expectations, safety standards, consumer information rights, hacking/terrorism risks, and job displacement.

Professionalism

  • Expertise in a specialized area (engineering, law, medicine, accounting)
  • Adherence to moral guidelines, usually formalized in a code of ethics

What is a Profession?

  • Sociological Account: A profession involves specialized knowledge and autonomy
  • Social Contract Account: Society grants privileges in exchange for service to the public good
  • Public Element: Professions serve the broader community, not just clients

Public Good & Well-Being

Engineers must hold paramount the safety, health, and welfare of the public. The Capabilities Approach (Sen & Nussbaum) defines well-being as access to food/shelter/water, satisfying human relationships, free movement and expression, and a satisfactory relationship with the natural world.

Three Types of Ethical Actions

  • Prohibited Actions: Actions forbidden by ethics codes
  • Preventing Harm: Identifying and disclosing potential harms, attempting to prevent them
  • Promoting Well-Being (Aspirational Ethics): Going beyond minimum requirements

Social Context of Technology

  • Engineering is a form of social experimentation
  • Technology affects society; society shapes technology development
  • Technological Determinism: Technology controls society
  • Technological Optimism: Technology will solve all problems
  • Technological Pessimism: Technology causes more harm than good
Ch. 2 A Practical Ethics Toolkit ▼
Heidelberg Cadaver Case

Engineers must hold paramount safety, health, and welfare — but does this override human dignity (e.g., using cadavers for crash testing)?

The Ethics Toolbox: Types of Issues

  • Factual Issues: Disagreements about what the facts actually are; sometimes cannot be fully resolved
  • Conceptual Issues: Disagreements about the meaning of terms (e.g., human dignity, informed consent, bribery, conflict of interest)
  • Application Issues: Whether a term or concept applies to a specific action or practice
  • Line Drawing: Resolving morally-laden application issues; sometimes must balance competing moral considerations

Conflicting Values: Creative Middle-Way Solutions

Key obligations to balance: public safety (Canon 1), faithful service to employer (Canon 4), and the engineer's own legitimate personal interests.

Common Morality

The stock of shared moral beliefs most people in a culture accept. The Universalization Principle states we must be willing for others to do what we do in similar situations.

Moral Theories

  • Utilitarianism: Maximize overall well-being. Use Cost-Benefit Test, Maximizing Good Consequences Test, and Rules & Practices Test
  • Respect for Persons (RP/Kantian): Act so you respect all humans as free and equal moral agents. Uses Golden Rule Test, Self-Defeating Test, and Rights Test
  • Virtue Ethics: Act as a good/virtuous person would. Virtue = dispositional trait (mean between two vices, per Aristotle)

Professional Virtues

  • Professional Care: Protect and promote well-being
  • Respect for Nature: Show consideration; avoid violation; treat with deference
Ch. 3 Responsibility in Engineering ▼
Columbia Shuttle Case

The Columbia disaster had two causes: a physical cause (foam strike) and organizational causes (culture of ignoring risk signals).

Types of Responsibility

  • Obligation-Responsibility: A duty to perform certain actions (e.g., follow standards)
  • Blame-Responsibility: Being held accountable for outcomes; involves causation

Standard of Care

Engineers are not liable for every error — society accepts "normal errors." The standard of care represents the highest shared standard among competent, responsible engineers in a given practice area. It goes beyond just following standard operating procedures.

Organizational Moral Responsibility

Philosopher Peter French argues organizations (corporations) can be morally responsible because they have: a decision-making mechanism, policies guiding decisions, and interests.

Impediments to Responsibility

Various factors can block engineers from acting responsibly, including organizational pressure, fear of retaliation, unclear lines of authority, and inadequate information.

Ch. 4 Engineers in Organizations ▼
Whistleblowing Focus

Engineers vs. Managers (Raelin)

There is a natural conflict due to differences in education, socialization, values, and work habits. Engineers often experience tension between loyalty to employer and loyalty to profession.

Jackall's Study of Managers

  • Managers prefer trade-offs between moral principles and utility
  • Loyalty to peers and superiors is the primary virtue
  • Lines of responsibility are deliberately blurred for self-protection
  • Organizational well-being measured primarily in financial terms

Recommendations for Ethical Organizations

  • Encourage employees to report bad news (critical loyalty)
  • Focus criticism on issues, not personalities
  • Keep written records of suggestions and complaints
  • Maintain confidentiality of complaints
  • Provide neutral outside participants
  • Protect against retaliation

Whistleblowing (DeGeorge)

Whistleblowing is morally permissible when: (1) harm is serious, (2) concerns are reported to immediate supervisors, (3) internal channels are exhausted. It is morally obligatory when: (4) documented evidence would convince a reasonable observer, and (5) going public will actually prevent the harm.

Davis's Complicity-Avoiding View

An engineer may be obligated to blow the whistle to avoid being complicit in wrongdoing, independent of whether it will prevent harm.

Ch. 5 Trust and Reliability ▼
Honesty & Integrity

Why Trust Matters

Society depends on professionals with specialized knowledge. The "knowledge explosion" creates an "ignorance explosion" — most people cannot evaluate technical claims themselves, so trust is essential.

Forms of Dishonesty

  • Lying: Stating something believed to be false with the intention to deceive
  • Deliberate Deception: Misleading through true statements arranged deceptively
  • Withholding Information: Omitting information the audience would reasonably expect, with intent to deceive
  • Failure to Seek Truth: Not making reasonable effort to verify claims

Why Dishonesty is Wrong (3 Frameworks)

  • Virtue Ethics: Honesty is a fundamental virtue; dishonesty is a character flaw
  • Respect for Persons: Deception violates the autonomy of moral agents by corrupting their decision-making
  • Utilitarian: Dishonesty undermines trust, which is essential for engineering and society to function

Research Integrity

  • Falsification of data
  • Fabrication of data
  • Failure to respect intellectual property
  • Plagiarism
  • Inappropriate attribution of authorship

Conflicts of Interest

A conflict between an obligation to exercise good judgment and an interest that may compromise that judgment. Note: it is not just any set of conflicting interests. A distinction exists between actual and potential conflicts of interest. Sometimes unavoidable — must be disclosed.
